Forest fires are spreading
By Paul Necasio (self-written)
Published: 26 December 2019
Updated: 29 December 2019

FOREST FIRES

A simple match can start a vast forest fire. At the start of the 1980s, the world is already aware that the Amazon rainforest is already on fire and currently in danger. Trees are being destroyed at a startling rate. From an area the size of Belgium, to New York, to California, the parts of the forest are being cut. This event captured the world's attention, or at least, the West, because the Amazon is home to half of the species of life on Earth and the forest is in theri hemisphere. Not only that but the Amazon also provides one-fifth of the oxygen we breathe and 20% of the world's freshwater. By the 1990s, the destruction accelerates and it seems like it was too late to save it. The rate of deforestation doubled. The destruction is becoming impossible to control and it may be gone...forever. See more.

Why is it burning? Between 1978 and 1988, an average of over 20,000 square kilometers was cut down each year and later became known as the Arc of Deforestation. People around the world demanded more meat and poultry which led to the rise of the demand for soybeans, the primary food for livestock because it's high in protein. Since Brazil grew one of the most amounts of soybeans, they joined in and took this opportunity to boost their economy. Small pastures were converted to massive soy farms. To add more farms, farmers pushed further into the Amazon rainforest and cut down trees (mostly illegal). The deforestation of the rainforest took cost for the boosting economy in beef and soy industries in Brazil. The arc then expanded further north sparking fierce resistance from environmental groups in Brazil.

Something changed by the 2000s. The annual destruction rate of the Amazon rainforest dropped by almost 70%. It was the lowest deforestation rate of the Amazon since the 21st century. The Amazon is starting to thrive and will be. But there was one problem, the conservation of the Amazon should be continuous, and the world didn't do that. The forest fire started burning again by 2010. Until now, the destruction rate increased exponentially, destroying the Amazon much further. Many actions have been taken by South Americans especially Brazil. Marina Silva was Brazil's Environment Minister in 2003 when she helped craft a plan to stop deforestation. At the time of the 2000s, only 28% of the forest is protected by the government. Business activities that destroy the forest were banned. More land was allotted to business that didn't destroy the forest like rubber-tapping and nut harvesting. The protected land was also added by the land of the indigenous people who preserve the forest. After these, half of the rainforest were protected. The government enforced the Forest Code which states that private landowners can only destroy and clear 20% of their private land. Their activities will be tracked and monitored by the forest service. IBAMA, a police agency, tracks and fine people who illegally cut parts of the rainforest. These actions were effective, dropping the rate of deforestation by half in 2006. Soy and beef industries felt pressured by the deforestation acts done by their consumers so they signed the Soy Moratorium. meaning they could continue to operate within existing farms, but they wouldn't buy soy from any newly deforested land in the Amazon. Other countries also gave donations to Brazil to help save the forest. See more.

Are we...done?Many ranchers, farmers, and landowners obeyed the law enforced by the environmentalists and the government on the Amazon. But this time, the Amazon is unlikely to survive another wave of deforestation. It's estimated that about 17% of the Amazon has been deforested in the last 50 years. A 2018 report estimated that, if the destroyed land reaches 20-25%, the whole rainforest could start to collapse. There won't be enough trees to cycle water for the whole rainforest and each tree will begin to dry out and die. These massacre of plants will produce carbon dioxide as they die out, further warming our planet. But in Brazil, many politicians and agricultural businesses continue to ignore the science for the sake of profit. And the world continues to ignore the reality for the sake of ...nothing. Clearing the Amazon for short-term gains overlooks the fact that the planet as we know it wouldn't exist without this rainforest. It’s why this place was saved once before and should be saved once more.
